Why time management matters
How managing your time well benefits you and the people around you
The opportunity
Your time is a resource
When you manage your time well, you feel more in control, less overwhelmed, and more satisfied with what you achieve each day. You're able to focus on work that genuinely matters — and deliver it to a standard you're proud of. Good time management isn't just something that benefits you, though. The people around you — your manager, your team, and other departments — all rely on you to show up, follow through, and communicate clearly. How you manage your time has a direct impact on them too.
A common pattern to watch for: treating all tasks as equally urgent. In reality, only a small portion of your work is both urgent and important. Learning to tell the difference is a core skill.

The priority matrix
A simple tool for sorting what deserves your attention
The framework
Urgent vs important
The priority matrix sorts every task on two axes: how urgent it is (does it need action today?) and how important it is (does it meaningfully contribute to goals?). Most people spend too much time in Quadrant 1 and Quadrant 3 — and not enough in Quadrant 2.
Urgent
Not urgent
Quadrant 1 — Do now
  • Deadline due today
  • Urgent request from a stakeholder
  • A live issue or crisis
See workplace examples
  • A report your manager needs for a 2pm leadership meeting
  • A pricing error on the website that's currently live
  • A colleague's system is down and they need your help to meet their deadline
  • An urgent customer escalation that needs a response today
Quadrant 2 — Schedule it
  • Long-term project work
  • Learning & development
  • Relationship building
See workplace examples
  • Preparing next quarter's strategy presentation
  • Completing this module or any scheduled training
  • A catch-up with a new team member or stakeholder
  • Reviewing and improving a recurring process
Quadrant 3 — Delegate or delay
  • Meetings where your input isn't critical
  • Some colleague requests
  • Non-urgent admin tasks
See workplace examples
  • A weekly team meeting where nothing on the agenda needs your input this week
  • A request that someone else on the team could handle equally well
  • Inbox organisation when nothing is time-sensitive
  • A report that's informational rather than action-driving
Quadrant 4 — Eliminate
  • Unnecessary browsing
  • Irrelevant email chains
  • Redundant reports
See workplace examples
  • Scrolling industry news that has no bearing on your work today
  • Staying on an email thread that resolved without your input
  • Reformatting a document no one reads
  • Attending a meeting that has no clear purpose or outcome
The goal: spend the majority of your time in Quadrant 2 — important but not yet urgent. This is where proactive, high-value work happens. Quadrant 1 should be the exception, not your default mode.

3
Planning your day
A practical structure for staying on track
The structure
Start with intention, not reaction
Opening your inbox and letting the day unfold is the path of least resistance — but it puts other people's priorities ahead of your own. A structured start, even just 10 minutes, sets the direction and reduces decision fatigue across the day.
☀
Morning review

Check your calendar, review tasks, and choose your top 3 priorities before opening email.

●
Eat the frog

A phrase coined by author Brian Tracy — the idea being that if you tackle your most important or dreaded task first thing, everything else feels easier. Energy and focus peak in the morning, so use that window wisely.

✉
Email in batches

Check email 2–3 set times per day rather than reacting to every notification.

◷
End-of-day capture

Note what's unfinished at close of day. Reduces mental load and makes tomorrow easier.

Rule of three: each day, identify the three tasks that — if completed — would make the day a success. Everything else is a bonus. This prevents the trap of busy but unproductive days.
Communication
When your plate is full — speak up early
One of the most important time management skills is raising concerns before a deadline is missed, not after. If a new task lands when you're already at capacity, it's professional to be transparent:

"I'd like to help with this. I currently have [X] due today and [Y] due tomorrow — would it be okay to pick this up on [day], or is there someone else who could assist sooner?"

4
Common time traps
Recognise the patterns that erode your focus
Trap 1
Perfectionism on low-stakes work
Spending excessive time polishing an internal email or reformatting a document that doesn't need it costs time that should go to higher-impact work. Ask yourself: "what level of quality does this task actually need?" Not everything deserves 100% effort.
Trap 2
Saying yes to everything
The instinct to be helpful and responsive is a strength — but overcommitting leads to missed deadlines and rushed work. A well-placed "yes, and here's when I can get to it" is far more reliable than an immediate yes that doesn't get delivered.
Trap 3
Confusing activity with progress
Being busy all day — but not moving key projects forward — is one of the most common traps. The measure of a productive day isn't hours of effort, it's meaningful output on the things that matter most.
Trap 4
Context switching without recovery
Jumping between tasks without pause loses cognitive momentum. Research shows it takes around 20 minutes to fully re-enter deep focus after an interruption. Where possible, group similar tasks together and protect uninterrupted blocks.
Trap 5
Starting work without clarity
Beginning a task before you fully understand what's needed — rather than asking questions upfront — often leads to rework, wasted effort, and frustration. It's always faster to ask for clarity at the start than to redo something at the end. If you're unsure of the brief, the expected output, or the deadline, ask before you begin.
Trap 6
Time creepers
Meetings booked for an hour that could be 30 minutes. Phone calls that drift well beyond their purpose. These "time creepers" quietly consume large chunks of your day. The fix is to set expectations upfront: "I have about 15 minutes for this call — let's make sure we cover what we need to." Being clear about your availability isn't rude — it's respectful of everyone's time.
Trap 7
Reactive notifications
Every ping, pop-up, and banner pulls your attention away from what you're doing. Even briefly checking a notification breaks your focus and extends the time a task takes. During periods of focused work, close unnecessary tabs, silence non-urgent notifications, and let people know you're heads-down. You don't have to be always-on to be effective.
Trap 8
Keeping it all in your head
Relying on memory to track tasks, deadlines, and commitments creates mental load and increases the risk of things being dropped. Writing things down — even a simple daily list — frees up mental space, reduces stress, and ensures nothing falls through the cracks. If it matters, capture it.
A useful question to ask yourself mid-task: "Is what I'm doing right now the best use of my time?" If the honest answer is no — pause, and redirect.
